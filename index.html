Chapter 01 · 2014 · CowTech FRC

The wooden robot.

High school. CowTech FRC — small-town FIRST Robotics team, almost no budget. Other teams arrived at regionals with aluminum chassis and CNC'd parts. We built our competition robot mostly out of wood: hand-cut plywood frame, bolt-together joints, zip ties holding pneumatics in place.

The wooden robot worked, against the odds. Without budget for raw stock, every part had to earn its place on the chassis. That season is where I learned to iterate fast and let go of ideas that weren't carrying weight.

The same instinct shows up now whenever a foreman tells me a feature doesn't work at 6 AM with one gloved thumb. The fix is almost never to add more — it's to take something out.

Chapter 02 · 2018+ · Danzel's Keyboard

Custom keyboards, first product.

The first thing I built and sold for money. Danzel's Keyboard started in late high school — soldering Cherry switches into hot-swap PCBs at the kitchen table, posting builds on r/MechanicalKeyboards, taking commissions through Discord. Every build was bespoke: switch type, keycap profile, sound dampening, lubed stabs, custom case, RGB underglow if you wanted it.

Cyberboard R4 "Gangstar Purple Thoughts" was the build that actually pushed me to figure out my margins. BOM math, drop-shipping, customs forms, returns. Selling to a hobbyist niche where the customer already knew exactly what they wanted is a fast way to learn what good documentation looks like — every build came with a wiring diagram and a typing-test video before it shipped.

Lesson from this era: if you can't explain the build to the customer in one paragraph, the product isn't done yet. That rule still runs every OpenYap tool.

Chapter 03 · 2018–22 · Cal Poly Pomona FSAE Electric

Suspension lead on
CPP's first EV race car.

At Cal Poly Pomona I joined the Formula SAE Electric team — a student-run program building a fully electric open-wheel race car from scratch. I led suspension: a-arm geometry, uprights, shock mounting, hub bearings, camber/caster setup, corner compliance testing.

This was CPP's first electric race car, which meant no prior-year chassis to steal from. We had to design for an unusual weight balance — battery pack dominating mass — and translate that into geometry that wouldn't wash out under cornering. Endless FEA iterations, CAD reviews, bolt-torque specs, tire data studies.

Building a race car runs on the same loop as shipping software — design, build, test, fix — except a missed tolerance means walking home from Michigan instead of redeploying. That season taught me to find the weakest part of any system first, and to stay honest about what I'd actually verified versus what I'd assumed.

Chapter 04 · 2022–23 · Clune Construction · Internship

The seed: AI for construction.

First time on a real construction site. Clune Construction — high-end commercial interiors, tight schedules, no slack for paperwork drift. The internship was supposed to teach me how a project actually moves. It did. It also taught me how much of a PE's day is information shuffling that no one is paid to redesign.

Submittals queued behind one engineer's inbox. RFIs answered three days later because someone had to walk three drawings to find the right detail. Schedule slips that no model would have caught. By the end of the summer, the idea was concrete: most of this is pattern matching that an AI could do faster than a human, if someone bothered to wire it up.

That's the seed every chapter after this is about cashing in. The next two years I'd spend asking working construction professionals if the idea held water — and then quietly building the first version on weekends.

Chapter 05 · 2024–25 · The pitch tour
record_voice_overWhere the idea got real

Selling the idea, on the road.

Started as a Cal Poly Pomona research project in February 2025: interview construction professionals about how they actually use AI — not how vendors say they should. Walked into a Webcor SF skyscraper interview, then chained walks across Las Vegas casino projects (Resorts World, Gentle Monster, Mirage, Durango), UC Irvine, then more SoCal and San Diego sites. Every conversation reshaped what was worth building.

CPP awarded me an office on campus to keep building the idea. The buildout never finished — the offer mattered more than the room. By the end of 2025 the research had compounded into a working system, and the next step was to take it to a real $2B jobsite.

01
Most of the learning happens on site, not in school.

A Webcor PE with a CM degree from Purdue: "My college experience was a mile wide and an inch deep. I didn't know any of the formwork systems, the sequences, the concrete pumping logistics. In almost two years at Webcor I've learned twice as much as in four years of college."

02
AI literacy is being set peer-to-peer, not by the curriculum.

The same engineer: "There was no professors talking about it. Nothing at the school… I only started using it because classmates were."

03
Office and field adopt at very different speeds.

A Cal Poly computational designer: "You have superintendents who are super good at what they do on site but want nothing to do with computers. And project managers in the office who love new technology. Same company, two different worlds."

04
Saving each role ten minutes a day.

"Even if it's as small as 10 minutes a day, 10 minutes a day adds up over months — that's a lot of money and time saved." Every OpenYap tool has to clear that bar before it ships.

05
Translation is unpaid work that the foreman absorbs.

A large share of LA-area crews speak Spanish as their first language. Every cost-code list, inspection checklist, and RFI reply gets translated in real time by the foreman, or it gets followed only halfway. Every OpenYap sheet now ships in English and Spanish from the start.

Chapter 06 · 2025 – present · Webcor Builders + OpenYap
starsGreatest work · so far

Webcor PE on a $2B site, shipping OpenYap on top.

Full-time Project Engineer at Webcor Builders on the LACC Convention Center Upgrade Program — a $2 billion modernization of the Los Angeles Convention Center. I run concrete operations across four sections: foundation pours, embed tracking, inspection coordination, takeoff, QC.

On top of the day job, OpenYap. Started with a single Telegram bot that nobody asked for, then a Discord agent the team actually used, then Mr. George (the construction agent), then CUP Dashboard (live pour tracking, footing maps, embed verification), then a launcher tying 14 internal apps together. Every tool ships against the same constraint: a foreman has to be able to operate it during a 6 AM tailgate meeting, gloves on, with the sun in his eyes.

The jobsite is unforgiving in a way office software never is. There are no rollbacks once a pour has started. The wrong reading at the wrong moment can cost six figures. That pressure is what keeps these tools honest.

Chapter 08 · Future · What we can do now
rocket_launchWE ARE JUST GETTING STARTED

This is the on-ramp.

The eight chapters above weren't the destination. They were the runway. With OpenYap on a real jobsite, the loop has gotten short enough that a new tool can go from "PM mentioned a problem on Friday" to "shipping into the trailer Monday."

The latest example: a concrete-ticket pipeline that ingests every truck ticket, normalizes the mix data, validates against the day's pour plan, and flags discrepancies before the slab is closed. Not a vendor demo — running on Section A this quarter. The same pattern works for material delivery, time tracking, embed inventory, inspection logging, anything the trailer touches.

Where this goes next: more agents trained on the field's actual language. More posters in trailers that don't have wifi. More tools that work with gloves on. More crews that walk away from a 12-hour day with paperwork already done. We are just getting started.
